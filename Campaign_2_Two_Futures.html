Six Principles to Choose Future A

Each one closes a specific gap between the two futures.

1
Recognize & Affirm Aging as a Natural Process
Not a disease. Should not contribute to ageism but increase opportunities for people to live the way they want as they age.
2
Increase Healthspan
Primary intention: increase the years of life in good health, not just total years. Lifespan gains are a welcome secondary benefit.
3
Ensure Access & Equity
Who benefits and who is left behind? Advances must be inclusive and beneficial for all — decrease, not increase, disparities.
4
Ensure Affordability for Individuals & the System
Costs must not be burdensome or limit access. Financial assistance must ensure affordability. Must produce savings and improve system sustainability.
5
Ensure Transparency
Consumer voices elevated. Needs of all populations considered in defining clinically relevant outcomes. Policymaking must be transparent and ensure meaningful changes in people's lives.
6
Utilize an Integrated Approach
Achievements must be fully integrated in, and remain part of, larger country-level efforts to address social determinants of health and longevity.

Design Notes

1
Side-by-side comparison format — A wide, two-column comparison where the layout itself makes the argument. Red (aspirational) vs blue (cautionary) — the reader sees both futures simultaneously.
2
Same data points, different outcomes — Each row compares the same dimension (access, healthspan, pricing, gaps) under two scenarios. The reader doesn't need explanation — the contrast does the work.
3
Principles as a 3×2 choice grid, not a list — Compact choice cards with alternating red/blue top borders — reinforcing that each principle is a decision between the two futures.

The Science

What is geroscience?

A field aiming to delay, prevent, alleviate, or reverse diseases of aging. The first clinical trials are already underway. The goal: more healthy years, not just more years. But without intentional choices, only the privileged few will benefit.

"Should not be limited to a wealthy few."
— AARP Geroscience Principles

Recognize & Affirm Aging as a Natural Process
The Choice
Geroscience should recognize that aging is a natural process, not a disease. It should not contribute to ageism but should instead increase opportunities for people to live the way they want as they age.
Future AAging honored across cultures. Science supports, not stigmatizes.
Future BAging treated as disease. Ageism deepens globally.
✕
Image
2
Increase Healthspan
The Choice
The primary intention of geroscience is to increase healthspan — the years of life that a person is in good health — rather than just the total years of life. Potential increases in lifespan are a welcome secondary gain.
Future AHealthy years increase for people in every country.
Future BMore years of life — but only for the wealthy. More years of disability for others.
✕
Image
3
Ensure Access & Equity
The Choice
Geroscience should help address the profound question of health disparities. Who will have access to and benefit from the emerging medical innovations of geroscience, and who will be left behind? Scientific advances must be inclusive and beneficial for all and must decrease rather than increase disparities.
Future ATrials in 40+ countries. Life expectancy gaps narrow.
Future BTrials in 12 wealthy nations. Gaps widen further.
✕
Image
4
Ensure Affordability for Individuals & the System
The Choice
The costs of geroscience interventions should not be burdensome to consumers or limit access to care. Sufficient financial assistance should be available to ensure affordability for all. These interventions, if meaningful and accessible, should produce cost savings, or at least a good cost-benefit proposition, and improve the financial sustainability of health care systems.
Future ATherapies priced for global health systems. Cost savings realized.
Future BTherapies priced for wealthy markets. New inequality driver.
✕
Image
5
Ensure Transparency
The Choice
Consumer voices must be elevated so the needs and concerns of all populations are taken into consideration when determining what constitutes a clinically relevant outcome. Policymaking must be transparent and engage the public to ensure that the outcomes represent meaningful changes in people's lives.
Future AAffected communities at the table. Public engagement drives policy.
Future BDecisions behind closed doors. Wealthy institutions decide for everyone.

1
Side-by-side future panels with ✓/✗ lists — The page literally splits in two. Aspirational on the left (✓ marks), cautionary on the right (✗ marks). The reader sees both futures simultaneously — the layout IS the argument.
2
Dual-future mini-cards on every principle — Each principle card has a teal/amber row at the bottom showing what happens under each future. "Future A: Trials in 40+ countries" vs "Future B: Trials in 12 nations." The comparison is embedded in every single card.

Alternating teal/amber card borders — Odd-numbered principles have teal top borders; even-numbered have amber. This visual rhythm reinforces that every principle is a choice between two futures.
